Technical Part 2 | Problem Solving

18 August 2024

Tell your non-tech friend a story about a time you: 1. were blocked on a simple problem:

Often a simple problem I get blocked on is syntax errors – not connecting CSS sheets, missing a comma, misspelling a variable etc. It can be frustrating when a large script won’t work when you’ve gone through the ‘logical’ checks and made sure your approach should work the way you intend it to. I learned that sometimes taking a short break, coming back to check all the simple things, in a manner that may seem arduous is incredibly important.

2. Elegantly Solved a Problem:

Not sure about ‘Elegantly’, but a recent problem I solved was creating one of my first for-loops that counted down “3! 2! 1! Blast Off!’. After only being introduced to Javascript for 3 days, this was quite difficult at first and my failure to set appropriate conditions led to my browser looping to 160,000+ lines before crashing. It was fun and exciting but it also got tiresome seeing failure after failure.

The approach I was giving back to the drawing board. I needed to reassess my understanding of for-loops and know how indexing works (requiring a little bit of humility). It felt demoralising at first because it seemed like I was taking 3 steps backwards – the “I should know this” mindset.

However, taking it back to the drawing board helped point me to a smaller detail that I had overlooked. After this, my for-loop worked as intended and I learned that sometimes its important to take a step back. In some ways, taking a step back is actually taking a step forward in the grand scheme of things.

3. Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes:

Pseudocode – I saw the effectiveness of planning code through comments from Joseph’s Javascript Cafe module. It was so effective, even for me to understand how a seemingly larger problem is chunked down by pseudocode.

Trying Something – Most of the time you just need to take a step forward to know, roughly, what you don’t know. Trying something is exceptionally helpful in initiating that process.

Rubber Ducky Method – Sometimes, when I explain a problem to someone, it gets the cogs in my brain moving and I start to think of different approaches to resolving my problems. This may be because the way you articulate the problem is different from the internal monologue in your brain.

Reading Error Messages – This is something I’m learning to do more of every day. The usefulness of this is beginning to show itself to me more as I journey through learning coding.

Console Logging – Still need to work on this but through the javascript modules, console logging has been the most in testing my functions to ensure they solve the problem.

Googling – This is a day-to-day problem solving task. I’m used to it and have developed a good understanding on the terms to use to find the right stack-overflow threads which describe my problems or any other useful websites for that matter (mozilla developer, w3 etc).

Asking for help – Sometimes I feel more reluctant to reach out for help as I seem to think I’m wasting someone else's time with something menial. I often do this last after exhausting all other a avenues.

Improving my Process with Reflection – I’ve been getting far better at pre-planning but I still often stray from times set, think I’m ahead of schedule but get rocked by a tough problem which puts me behind etc. However, I’m constantly improving my process and getting so much better.
